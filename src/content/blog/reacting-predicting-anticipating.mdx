Does your L&D team talk about being "more strategic"? What does that mean in practice?

The word *strategic* has been thrown around so much that it now signals aspiration instead of specifying behaviour.

So let's warm up the conversation with something more useful. We'll do so by looking at three positions an L&D function can hold. They are three genuinely different ways of relating to the future and working in the now, and only one of them really gets to what most people are talking about when they say *strategic*.

## Reacting: at everyone's whim

The reactive function waits. A manager submits a training request. A new system gets rolled out and someone realises, late, that nobody knows how to use it. A compliance deadline appears on the calendar. Each of these lands on L&D's desk as a fully-formed demand, and the team's job is to fulfil it as fast as possible.

This is the default state for a lot of L&D functions. When you react, you are always downstream of decisions already made elsewhere. You don't shape the requirement; you receive it. You don't choose your priorities; the inbox chooses them for you. You are, structurally, at the whim of whoever happens to ask loudest or latest.

The reactive function is also permanently behind. By the time a need surfaces as a request, the business could have been living with the gap for months. The system launched without training. The new hires struggled through onboarding that didn't exist. The capability problem became visible only once it became painful. L&D arrives to clean up after the fact, and, because the work was reactive, gets measured on speed of delivery rather than quality of outcome. You become an order-taker with a queue, and order-takers don't get a seat at the table where the orders are decided.

The cruelest part is that reactive teams often work extremely hard. The problem isn't effort or skill. It's position.

## Predicting: the educated guess that isn't

The instinctive escape from reaction is prediction. If being downstream is the problem, then surely the answer is to get ahead, to forecast what the business will need and build it in advance. This is what most people mean when they say "strategic L&D." It feels like the opposite of reactive.

But prediction has a hidden flaw: a prediction is a claim about a specific future state, and specific claims about the future are mostly wrong. "The sales team will need negotiation training in Q3." "We're going to grow the engineering org, so we should build a technical leadership pipeline now." "Digital transformation means everyone will need data literacy by next year." These sound strategic. They are guesses, often confident ones, often dressed in the language of foresight, but guesses all the same.

Predictions go wrong constantly, and they go wrong in expensive ways. The reorganisation that was definitely happening gets shelved. The product line you built capability around gets discontinued. The growth you forecast arrives in a different function than expected, or arrives as contractors rather than employees, or doesn't arrive at all. When L&D commits real budget and months of build time to a predicted future, a wrong prediction isn't just a miss. It's sunk cost, wasted capacity, and a credibility hit that pushes the function right back into reactive defensiveness.

The deeper issue is epistemic. A prediction treats the future as a single point you're trying to hit. But organisational futures aren't points; they're distributions of possibility that shift as conditions change. Betting the function on hitting one point is a worse risk than reacting, because at least the reactive team isn't spending money on the wrong thing in advance. Prediction is reaction with extra confidence and a longer lead time on the mistake.

## Anticipating: building the sensing capability

Anticipation is different in kind, not just degree. Where the predictor stakes a claim on one future, the anticipator builds the capability to read many futures as they develop, and to move early when the signal becomes strong enough to act on.

The distinction is the difference between a weather forecaster who declares in January that it will rain on the third Tuesday in June, and a sailor who watches the sky, reads the barometer, feels the wind shift, and adjusts the sails before the squall hits. The forecaster is making a prediction and will probably be wrong. The sailor is anticipating. Not by knowing exactly what will happen, but by having the instruments, the attention, and the practised judgement to respond to what is actually emerging.

For an L&D function, anticipation means building two things the reactive and predictive teams lack.

The first is **sensing infrastructure**: feelers out in the business and sensors tuned to the signals that precede a capability need. This is concrete, not mystical. It means the L&D lead sits in or has visibility of strategic planning conversations, not just after decisions are announced. It means relationships with line leaders deep enough that they think aloud with you about what's coming, not just what's broken. It means watching leading indicators, hiring plans, system procurement, new market entries, regulatory shifts, shifts in the work itself, because every one of those is a capability need before it is a training request. It means paying attention to attrition patterns, to the questions showing up in support queues, to what high performers are quietly teaching each other. The signals are already in the organisation; reactive teams just aren't positioned to receive them.

The second is **interpretive judgement**: the practised skill to read those signals well. Data alone doesn't anticipate. Plenty of teams drown in dashboards and still get surprised. Anticipation is the developed ability to distinguish a weak signal worth watching from noise, to recognise the early shape of a pattern, and to hold several plausible futures in mind at once while staying ready to act on whichever one starts to harden into fact. This is a skill the function builds deliberately, the way the sailor builds it: through exposure, through reflection, through being wrong in small ways often enough to get calibrated.

Anticipation doesn't require certainty. It requires positioning and readiness. You don't need to know exactly what's coming; you need to be close enough to the business, and sensitive enough to its signals, that you see the shape of it forming while there's still time to prepare.

## What anticipation buys you

Anticipation changes what an L&D function is allowed to do and what it gets to become.

**You initiate instead of waiting.** The anticipatory function doesn't sit in the inbox waiting for requests. It generates its own work from what it sees coming. When you can read the signals, you can propose the leadership programme before the growth that needs it, design the onboarding before the hiring wave, build the capability before the gap becomes painful. You set your own priorities because you saw the need first.

**You approach the business before the business knows it has a problem.** This is the move that changes the relationship entirely. Instead of a line leader coming to you with a fully-formed (and often mis-specified) training request, you go to them: *"I've noticed the support team's ticket volume is shifting toward this new product area, and your headcount plan suggests you're growing that team next quarter. Should we get ahead of the capability need now?"* That conversation positions L&D as a partner who sees around corners, not a vendor who fulfils orders. It is the single fastest route to the credibility that "strategic" gestures at.

**You stop wasting money on wrong bets.** Because anticipation is about readiness rather than committed prediction, you're not sinking budget into one forecast future. You watch, you prepare lightly, and you commit hard only when the signal is strong. You get the lead time of prediction without the sunk-cost risk.

**You earn the seat at the table.** The reactive team is measured on throughput. The predictive team is measured on whether its bets paid off, a brutal scorecard. The anticipatory team is measured on its judgement and its foresight, which is exactly the currency that buys influence over strategy. When you consistently see needs before others do, people start including you in the conversations where needs are created. That's the seat. You don't get it by asking; you get it by demonstrating that you already see what they're about to face.

**You change the function's identity.** Reactive L&D is a cost centre that produces courses. Anticipatory L&D is a capability function that helps the business navigate its own future. That's not a rebrand. It's a different job, and it's a far more defensible one when budgets get scrutinised.

## The move that matters

The progression isn't reactive → predictive → anticipatory, as though prediction were a necessary waystation. Prediction is a detour. A more confident, more expensive form of being wrong. The real move is from reacting to anticipating, and it's a move about position and capability, not about getting better at guessing.

Get close to where the business is heading. Build the feelers and the sensors. Develop the judgement to read what they pick up. Then act early, on signal, before the request arrives. That's what "strategic" actually means when you make it concrete, and it's the only one of the three postures that puts L&D in charge of its own work.
